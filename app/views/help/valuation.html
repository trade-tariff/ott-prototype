What is customs valuation?

All goods traded international have a customs value. This is declared to customs authorities together with other customs and product information.

An introduction to valuation

Watch our short video to understand more about valuing your goods for customs.

 

At import, that value is the basis on which customs duty, import VAT and other import-related taxes are calculated. If incorrect customs value is declared at import, it will result in underpayment of duty and import VAT. HMRC may then request the company to pay the difference.

At export, customs value is mainly used for trade statistics.

Customs valuation follows international rules and best practice. This allows for consistency and harmonisation in international trade.

In the majority of cases, the value listed on the commercial invoice between the exporter and importer will form the basis for customs valuation. This is referred to as transaction value and is defined as the price actually paid or payable for the goods sold for export. Additional rules apply to related companies to ensure that the relationship between entities does not affect valuation and as a result, the amount of duty and import VAT due.

In rare cases, there will be no transaction accompanying import and export, for example when moving companyâ€™s own stock. In such cases, alternative valuation methods will need to be used.

The transaction value would then need to be adjusted based again on international rules.

Certain costs such as transport and insurance up to the point of arrival at the UK border need to be added to the value. Royalties, license fees and some types of commissions will also need to be added.

On the other hand, transport costs within the country of import, for example, the UK, as well as import duties, can be subtracted.

Who is responsible for determining the customs value of products?

Importers and exporters are responsible for determining the correct value. However, in the case of transport costs or insurance, for example, the final costs may not be available upfront. It may be necessary to liaise with the freight forwarder to confirm the final value of all amounts that need to be added or subtracted from the transaction value.

The importer and exporter remain liable for the correctness of the customs value declared to HMRC. This is the case even when the company uses an intermediary to declare goods to customs on their behalf. The company would also be liable for any underpayment of duty or other taxes resulting from incorrect value declared.
